## Dashboard Design Process

We set out to create a comprehensive analytical dashboard focusing on the relationship between poverty, obesity, and fast-food density in each region. We wanted to show this information in a way that was easy to digest while also including some light statistics in the form of correlation analysis. It was our thought that if we could show the relationships between these three factors and then map that risk across the country, we could not only show how these variables interact with each other but also predict troubled areas for intervention. 

We started with this basic concept and a small set of data sources that showed our idea may be possible. We then created a basic wireframe of what the dashboard would eventually look like. This included key performance indicators on the top to immediately grab users’ attention with key details and then continuing with more specific visualizations as the dashboard continues. This structure would allow us to introduce our users to the problem statement very generally and then progressively take them through the data towards a more complete and nuanced view of the issue at hand. We wanted the dashboard to work like a funnel, bring the users in and provide them a holistic overview before eventually exposing them to the harsh realities of poverty and health in the United States. 

The topic we are exploring here is incredibly serious and not particularly positive, to communicate this both appropriately within the context of the situation as well as adhering to Gestalt principles of design the dashboard is primarily color coded in various shades of red. The intensity of red demonstrates severity of obesity, poverty, fast food density, or even the concept of health risk itself. The dashboard also explores the fast-food culprits themselves at the bottom of the dashboard but uses a wider range of colors to more clearly denote them. Additionally, all the data shown in visualizations are controlled for population size. Had we merely passed raw data into our visualizations rather than rely on calculated columns to normalize the information we would have seen incredibly skewed results that would indicate that large population centers are bad and rural areas are good. This would not only be far too simplistic of an analysis method, but it would also completely fail to answer what the relationship between these factors truly is. 

## Data Preparation
1. **Deduplication & Standardization:** Cleaning fast-food chain nomenclature (e.g., standardizing `McDonalds`, `McDonald's`, and `mcdonalds`) to ensure proper grouping.
2. **Spatial Join / Aggregation:** Translating point-level coordinate data (Latitude/Longitude) into unique US County FIPS codes using GIS tools / Tableau spatial blending. Extrapolate additional datapoints to create larger more complete dataset overall. Our final dataset had over 25k rows.
3. **Feature Engineering:** Creating calculated fields for population-normalized metrics within Tableau:
   $$\text{Fast Food Density} = \left( \frac{\text{Total Restaurants}}{\text{County Population}} \right) \times 10,000$$

## Project Limitations

The most substantial limitation we face is data accuracy and relevance which is a direct byproduct of our limited ability to source reliable data on this scale. The concept for our project is something that has been explored before in the real world and there are datasets that support it but many of these datasets cost thousands of dollars. It was very straight forward to source US Census data on county names, FIPs codes, obesity rates per capita and location but extremely challenging to find relevant and accurate fast-food data on a national scale. For that reason, it was necessary for us to adapt free datasets available publicly online at the cost of realism and accuracy. This can be clearly observed in our Poverty vs Obesity trend analysis; the data is nearly perfectly correlated. This would never happen in real life and is a by-product how we had to scale our data. Similarly, the extrapolated data source produced an incredibly obvious hot spot on the southeastern region of the United States from our main risk map. It appears the LLM had its own biases on what the data should ultimately look like in the end and singled out the southeast region to be incredibly impoverished, obese, and unhealthy. 

## Assumptions
* The data is relevant enough and accurate enough to make realistic conclusions about the actual relationship between obesity, poverty, and fast-food density.
* That county level is sufficient to observe statistically significant trends in data.
* That our composite risk score that has been calculated through weighting normalized obesity rates, fast food density, and poverty measures is an accurate and predictive way of determining a counties risk profile.

## Application of Class Principles

The team’s most important takeaway is that normalization and data structure matter more than raw visual complexity. We used Gestalt principles of similarity and proximity by keeping all risk-related views in a consistent color family and grouping related charts close together, so users can naturally connect the map, scatterplot, and ranked county views as part of one story. Additionally, we used visual hierarchy by placing the macro geographic view first, then the analytical correlation views, and finally the county/category drill-down views. We used preattentive attributes such as darker color intensity for higher-risk counties and larger mark size for higher fast-food density so that extreme cases stand out before users even read the labels. Lastly, we applied exploratory vs. explanatory balance by making the dashboard interactive enough for drill-down, but still structured around a clear narrative: where risk is highest, what factors are associated with it, and what actions might follow.
